# Chapter 13: Behavioral and Emotional Needs

## Learning Objectives
- Recognize emotional responses in residents
- Provide supportive care for anxiety and depression
- Respond appropriately to challenging behavior
- Encourage independence with empathy
- Communicate with families respectfully
- Know when to report mental health concerns

---

## 13.1 Emotional Well-Being
Emotional health is part of total wellness.

Residents may experience:
- Sadness or grief
- Anxiety or fear
- Loneliness or isolation
- Frustration over loss of independence
- Confusion or worry

Support with kindness, patience, and active listening.

---

## 13.2 Managing Anxiety and Depression
Observe and report emotional changes.

Helpful actions:
- Offer reassurance and calm presence
- Encourage social interaction
- Report symptoms like withdrawal or crying
- Help maintain routines
- Avoid minimizing feelings

Good emotional support can improve quality of life.

---

## 13.3 Responding to Behavior Changes
Behavior may signal unmet needs.

Examples:
- Agitation may mean pain or fear
- Repetition may be a request for attention
- Aggression may be frustration or confusion
- Withdrawal may be depression or fatigue

Respond with calm redirection and report concerns to the nurse.

---

## 13.4 Supporting Independence
Encourage residents to do as much as they can safely.

Support independence by:
- Offering choices
- Allowing time for self-care
- Encouraging participation in daily activities
- Avoiding doing everything for them
- Recognizing achievements

This approach preserves dignity and self-esteem.

---

## 13.5 Family Communication
Family involvement can be a support.

Communicate by:
- Listening to family concerns
- Sharing factual observations when appropriate
- Respecting family wishes within policy
- Referring medical questions to the nurse
- Helping family members feel included

Clear communication helps families feel supported.
