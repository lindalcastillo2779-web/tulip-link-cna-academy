# Chapter 14: Care of the Dying and Post-Mortem Care

## Learning Objectives
- Recognize signs of approaching death
- Provide comfort to dying residents
- Support families during end-of-life care
- Understand post-mortem care procedures
- Maintain dignity after death
- Manage your emotions professionally

---

## 14.1 Recognizing End-of-Life Changes
Near the end of life, residents may experience:
- Increased sleepiness or decreased responsiveness
- Irregular breathing or periods of apnea
- Reduced appetite and fluid intake
- Cool, pale extremities
- Restlessness or agitation

These changes should be reported to the nurse and handled with compassion.

---

## 14.2 Comfort Care
Comfort is the top priority.

Provide care by:
- Keeping the resident clean and dry
- Repositioning gently as needed
- Offering mouth care and lip balm
- Reducing noise and unnecessary lighting
- Speaking softly and reassuringly

Comfort care supports dignity and peace.

---

## 14.3 Family Support
Family needs information and support.

Helpful actions:
- Listen to family concerns
- Allow privacy when requested
- Offer to notify staff of needs
- Help with seating or refreshments
- Be respectful of family rituals

Family support is an important part of end-of-life care.

---

## 14.4 Post-Mortem Care
After death, the body is cared for respectfully.

Post-mortem steps often include:
- Positioning the body with the head slightly elevated
- Closing the eyes and mouth gently
- Removing tubes and equipment per policy
- Covering the body with a clean sheet
- Documenting the time of death as directed

Treat the body with the same dignity as when the person was alive.

---

## 14.5 Coping with Caregiver Emotions
Caring for dying residents can be emotional.

Caregiver strategies:
- Talk with a trusted coworker or supervisor
- Use facility support resources
- Take a moment to breathe and ground yourself
- Respect your feelings without judgment
- Continue providing professional care

Taking care of yourself helps you care for others.
